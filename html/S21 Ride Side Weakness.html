21. Right Side Weakness

You are called emergent to a 61 year old male patient who is having right sided weakness. The patient is pale and diaphoretic on arrival. Patient is AAOX4 and says that he was watching TV about 30 minutes ago and suddenly felt weak on the right side. Patient states that he has never felt like this before and gives you a list of medications.
  • What are the MOST important questions to ask before alerting the hospital and performing a Cincinnati stroke scale?

    1. Complete SAMPLE history
    2. Do you have a history of seizures or diabetes?
    3. Are you in pain?
    4. Do you have a history of heart or lung problems?

  • The correct answer is 2. Do you have a history of seizures or diabetes?

    Although all the questions are important, seizures can mimic stroke (Todd's Paralysis), and hypo/hyperglycemia can mimic stroke.

This patient presents with a positive Cincinnati stroke scale (positive for arm drift and slurred speech). You are very concerned about a hemorrhagic stroke causing increased intracranial pressure. You check vitals at 180/110, R 72, respirations 12. You check BGL and this patient has a BGL of 49.
  • What should you do about this low BGL?

    1. Administer D50
    2. Withhold D50 due to the increased risk of brain tissue damage
    3. Give some D50 but drip it in slow
    4. Call medical direction for advice

  • The correct answer is 1. Administer D50.

    You could always call for advice, but if the patient is hypoglycemic then they should get D50.

  • How can you distinguish a stroke vs Bell's Palsy?

    1. If they have slurred speech, then it is Bell's Palsy.
    2. Ask the patient to wrinkle their forehead. If their forehead wrinkles unilaterally (one side only) they likely have Bell's Palsy.
    3. Ask the patient to wrinkle their forehead. If their forehead wrinkle's up bilaterally then they have Bell's Palsy.
    4. There is no way to tell the difference between the two in the field.

  • The correct answer is 3. Ask the patient to wrinkle their forehead. If their forehead wrinkle's up bilaterally then they have Bell's Palsy.

    Bell's Palsy is paralysis of cranial nerve 7, the facial nerve, and because of the paralysis the forehead will be unable to wrinkle on one side as the nerve impulse has been cut off lower down the nerve tract. With a stroke, the cranial nerve will not be paralysed in this way and the patient will retain the ability to wrinkle their forehead bilaterally. Obviously arm drift or other signs excludes the possibility of Bell's Palsy.

  • When you perform the Cincinnati stroke scale, what type of arterial occlusion does it test for?

    1. Occlusion of the intervertebral artery
    2. Occlusion of the middle cerebral artery
    3. Occlusion of the posterior cerebral artery
    4. Occlusion near the circle of Willis

  • The correct answer is 2. Occlusion of the middle cerebral artery.

    An occlusion of the middle cerebral artery is the most common and this is what the Cincinnati stroke scale is designed to test.
