23. DNR

You are called emergent to a private residence where a patient has fallen. The patient is an 89 year old male who fell in the hallway. There is a CNA on scene who states that the patient called out to her saying he didn’t feel normal and then slumped to the floor. CNA states that patient has significant medical history and goes to find a list.

Patient has agonal respirations and pulse of 30 bpm, on arrival. Patient is in a position that you cannot provide adequate care, so you move him to the living room while holding C-Spine precautions. The CNA comes back from the kitchen and hands you a stack of papers and says the patient has a DNR. You quickly read through the DNR and it states no CPR or positive pressure ventilation should be used. The nurse appears nervous and states she didn’t want this to happen while she is there.

  • What should you do at this time?

    1. Call medical direction for orders
    2. Start providing medical care since you moved the patient to the living room
    3. Provide no care and transport to the hospital
    4. Apply Combi-patches and pace.

  • The correct answer is 1. Call medical direction for orders.

    This is an end of life care issue and medical direction should be called. In this case, the patient is clear that no CPR or ventilation should be performed, and these procedures should not be started.

You contact medical direction and they ask you to obtain baseline vitals and then run an EKG on the patient. Your vitals are respirations 4/minute, pulse 40, BP unknown. This is the strip that you see:

    • What is the name of this rhythm strip?

      1. Sinus bradycardia
      2. Junctional escape
      3. IVR
      4. Ectopic atrial rhythm

    • The correct answer is 1. Sinus bradycardia.

      Take a look at lead II and you can see some P wave shapes before the QRS complex.

    • Medical direction asks you to take no action with this patient, but states you can transport if you like. The EKG continues to read 30-40 BPM in a bradycardic rhythm but now no pulse can be felt. This goes on for about 10-15 minutes. The patient then becomes Asystolic. What should you do at this time?

      1. Start CPR
      2. Call the county coroner
      3. Call medical direction for a time of death
      4. Leave the patient with a police officer on scene

    • The correct answer is 3. Call medical direction for a time of death.

      You will need to obtain a time of death from a doctor. Explain that the patient has been in PEA for some time and is now noted as Asystole in 3 leads.
