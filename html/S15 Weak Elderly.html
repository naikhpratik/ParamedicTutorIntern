15. Weak Elderly

You are called emergent to an 80 year old male patient who has been feeling very weak. Patient states he started feeling this way yesterday and thought he was just getting sick. Patient denies nausea and vomiting and states that he has been able to eat and drink like normal. You check the patient's vital signs at pulse 44, BP 86/p, respirations 12 unlabored, SPO2 90% on room air. Patient denies pertinent cardiac or respiratory history. You place the patient on a 4 lead and this is the EKG that you see:

    • Why is this patient feeling so light-headed?

      1. Patient is bradycardic
      2. Patient is having an increase in vagal tone causing bradycardia
      3. Patient is becoming septic
      4. Patient has a heart block and slow ventricular rate

    • The correct answer is 4. Patient has a heart block and slow ventricular rate.

      This patient has a heart block, 2 degree Type 2 and the heart block is causing bradycardia.

    • What is the best treatment plan to resolve this type of heart block and maintain adequate pressure?

      1. Pace
      2. Atropine 0.5mg up to 3mg
      3. Epi drip
      4. Dopamine drip

    • The correct answer is 1. Pace.

      This patient should be sedated with Benzodiazepines if allowed, then paced. It is unlikely that atropine will be effective on this type of heart block.

    • Why would atropine not be effective on this type of heart block?

      1. Atropine is contraindicated on 2nd degree type 2 heart blocks because it can cause a 3rd degree block.
      2. Atropine only works on the ventricles and the atria would stay at the same rate.
      3. Atropine blocks innervation of the parasympathetic nervous system and will increase rate at the SA node but ventricular rate with be unaffected due to the heart block.
      4. Innervation of the sympathetic nervous system can be depressed by giving atropine.

    • The correct answer is 3. Atropine blocks innervation of the parasympathetic nervous system and will increase rate at the SA node but ventricular rate with be unaffected due to the heart block.

      Atropine is an anticholinergic and will block the parasympathetic nervous system at the atria, but it cannot increase the ventricular heart rate too greatly because there is a heart block present.

    • You sedate the patient with 5mg midazolam and begin to pace this patient by applying the defibrillation patches and turning on the pacer. You notice that there is no change to heart rate. What should you do ensure capture is obtained?

      1. Turn off the pacer and defibrillate
      2. Turn up the amperage
      3. Turn up the rate
      4. Give epinephrine IM to boost cardiac output

    • The correct answer is 2. Turn up the amperage.

      Turning up the amperage is the only way to ensure capture is obtained.

    • When capture is obtained by turning up the amperage, you will be able to tell by:

      1. There will be QRS complexes after the pacer spikes
      2. Blood pressure will improve
      3. You will be able to feel a palpable pulse
      4. All of the answers are correct

    • The correct answer is 4. All of the answers are correct.

      Looking for the large, bizarre shaped QRS complexes after pacing will ensure that pacing is working. Checking for mechanical pulse and BP improvement is the next step.
