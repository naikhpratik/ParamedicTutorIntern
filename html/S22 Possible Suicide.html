22. Possible Suicide

You are called emergent to a 35 year old male patient for a possible suicide attempt. Family on scene state that patient appears to have taken some pills but they are not sure what they are. Family states patient has been struggling with depression for a while. The pills are up stairs and you send your partner up to get a medication list. Your partner comes back with 3 empty pill bottles and states nothing else abnormal was found. Family report no other problems besides depression, no heart or lung problems, no alcohol abuse. The bottles contained, Erythromycin, Amitriptyline, Zoloft and now they are all empty.

The patient is currently lethargic and responds with moaning to most questions. Patient withdrawals from pain. Patient moves all extremities without difficulty. Vitals are pulse 150, BP 122/60, respirations 15. You place the patient on the cardiac monitor and this is the strip that you see:

    • What is your primary concern with this patient?

      1. Patient may become unstable due to overdose
      2. Patient may need airway control
      3. Patient could be having a cardiac problem
      4. All answers are correct

    • The correct answer is 4. All answers are correct.

      At this time, the patient is an unknown overdose and should be treated as such. This wide complex tachycardia could be the result of an overdose or it could be causing the altered mentation.

    • What would you check next on this patient?

      1. BGL
      2. SPO2
      3. 12 lead
      4. See if patient can follow simple commands

    • The correct answer is 1. BGL.

      This patient is presenting with altered mentation and you should check BGL, because this cause is easily reversible. The others are good options too, but not as important at this time.

    • BGL comes back at 132mg/dl. Patient is still responding but seems to be doing worse. Based on the medication list, this patient could be suffering from:

      1. Narcotic overdose
      2. Tricyclic antidepressant overdose
      3. Sepsis
      4. Patient has old cardiomyopathy

    • The correct answer is 2. Tricyclic antidepressant overdose.

      The patientâ€™s medication list contains Amitriptyline which is a TCA medication. If the patient is suffering from an overdose of TCA, this could explain the odd looking EKG.

    • What medication can be used to reverse these side effects of TCA overdose?

      1. Narcan
      2. Epinephrine 1:10
      3. Sodium Bicarbonate
      4. Calcium

    • The correct answer is 3. Sodium Bicarbonate.

      The medication used for suspected TCA overdose is Sodium Bicarbonate.
